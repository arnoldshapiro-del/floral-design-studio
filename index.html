Design School

7 Principles of Floral Design

Master these 7 principles and you'll design like a professional. What Team Flower's expensive education platform teaches â€” free.

1. Balance

Visual weight distributed evenly. Symmetrical = formal elegance. Asymmetrical = modern, artistic. Use heavier blooms (roses, peonies) low and center. Lighter flowers (sweet peas, grasses) at the edges.

2. Proportion

The arrangement should be 1.5-2Ã— the height of its container. Flowers should relate to each other in size â€” don't put a tiny spray rose next to an enormous dahlia without transitional sizes between.

3. Rhythm

Your eye should flow naturally through the design. Create rhythm by repeating colors, shapes, or textures at intervals. Place the same flower in 3 spots to create a visual triangle.

4. Focal Point

Every arrangement needs ONE star. Place your most stunning bloom slightly off-center and lower than other flowers. The eye should land here first, then travel outward.

5. Contrast

Mix opposites: light/dark, large/small, round/spiky, smooth/textured. Contrast creates visual interest. A monochrome arrangement needs extra texture contrast to avoid looking flat.

6. Harmony

All elements should feel like they belong together. A wildflower meadow bouquet shouldn't include a formal orchid. Match flower personality to the overall style and occasion.

7. Unity

The arrangement reads as one composition, not individual flowers stuck together. Overlap stems, nestle blooms close, let greenery weave through. The finished piece should feel organic and intentional.

2026 Trends

Floral Design Trends 2026

What's hot this year in the floral world. Stay ahead of every trend â€” knowledge that Florists' Review subscription provides.

Meadow-to-Vase

Wild, gathered, just-picked-from-the-field look. Loose, asymmetric, with unexpected elements like grasses, herbs, and seed pods. Anti-perfection is the new perfection.

Monochromatic Luxe

All one color in many shades. All-white, all-blush, or all-burgundy. Focuses on texture variation instead of color contrast. Ultra-sophisticated.

Sustainability Focus

Locally grown, seasonal-only, no floral foam. Chicken wire mechanics, reusable vessels. Clients asking about carbon footprint of imported flowers.

Dried + Fresh Mix

Pampas grass with garden roses. Dried bunny tails with fresh peonies. The contrast of preserved and living flowers creates incredible texture and visual interest.

Terracotta & Earth Tones

Warm, earthy palettes replacing the cool blush tones of recent years. Think rust, cinnamon, caramel, and sage â€” inspired by desert landscapes and natural clay.

Floral Installations

Giant hanging clouds of flowers, floral chandeliers, flower walls, and ceiling installations. The "wow factor" that makes events go viral on social media.

Garden Rose

7-10 days
Water: Change daily. Remove all foliage below waterline.
Temperature: Cool room, 65-72Â°F. Keep away from direct sun.
Cut: Diagonal cut, 1 inch. Re-cut every 2 days.
Design Tip: Opens beautifully from tight bud. Buy 2-3 days before event. Remove guard petals for full bloom look.
Pairs With: Peonies, ranunculus, eucalyptus, sweet peas

Peony

5-7 days
Water: Fresh cool water daily. Use flower food.
Temperature: Cool room. Refrigerate to slow opening, warm room to speed up.
Cut: Diagonal cut. Buy in "marshmallow" stage (soft but not open).
Design Tip: The #1 most-requested wedding flower. Book 3-6 months in advance for peak season. Available April-June only.
Pairs With: Garden roses, ranunculus, sweet peas, jasmine vine

Dahlia

3-5 days
Water: Shallow water (2-3 inches). Change daily. Hollow stems are fragile.
Temperature: Cool. They wilt fast in heat. Mist lightly.
Cut: Straight cut into hot water for 30 seconds, then cold water.
Design Tip: Short vase life â€” design last. Cafe au Lait is the most Instagrammed variety. Huge impact for photos.
Pairs With: Garden roses, cosmos, amaranthus, olive branches

Hydrangea

7-14 days
Water: HEAVY drinker. Deep water. Submerge heads to revive if wilted.
Temperature: Cool. Mist petals regularly. They absorb through petals.
Cut: Smash woody stem ends with hammer. Cross-cut the base.
Design Tip: One stem fills an entire vase. Best volume-per-dollar flower. Great for budget-conscious designs.
Pairs With: Roses, peonies, delphiniums, stock

Tulip
